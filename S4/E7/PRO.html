  1. Weigh the inner cup of the calorimeter. Note the metal with which the inner cup of the calorimeter is made and note down the specific heat capacity of this material from the physical constants table.
  2. Determine what mass of water would be required to fill the inner cup 1/3rd or 1/4th full.
  3. Do a trial calculation to determine what mass of shot at 1000C must be added to cold water at 50 below room temperature to give a final equilibrium temperature about 50 above room temperature. Since you don’t yet know the specific heat of the metal you have to make an intelligent guess of its value for this calculation. Consult handbook values for metals, but keep in mind the possibility that the sample you have been given just might be an alloy whose value is not in the table. We want to achieve a temperature change of at least 40C in water, without using so much shot that the cup becomes overfull.
  4. Now that you have determined how much shot to use, weigh out that much. Put it in the steam-generator cup, plug the top of the cup with cotton, and heat the shot in the steam-generator.
  5. Typical weight to get a reasonable temperature change is ~ 80 – 100 gm of the shot and take the water having 4 – 50C lower temperature than that of room temperature. The temperature of the water can be lowered by adding appropriate amount of either ice-water or ice. In this case, the total weight of the water is weight of water at room temperature and that of ice-water/ice. Put the cold water in the inner cup weigh both together. Assemble the calorimeter. Allow five minutes time for the calorimeter and water to attain equilibrium. Note down initial temperature of water in the calorimeter, Tw.
  6. When the shot cup has been in the steam bath for at least 10 minutes the metal shot will be at the steam temperature (near 1000 C depending on the barometric pressure). Ensure that the shots have reached steady state temperature. Note down this temperature, Ts.
  7. Quickly transfer the shot and reassemble it, then continue your temperature readings of the water in the calorimeter every ½ minute. The exact time of dumping in the shot must be noted also. Take temperature readings for six minutes after the maximum temperature, Tf, is observed. This helps to clearly establish the cooling curve of the water.
  8. Following the procedure outlined in para entitled ‘cooling correction due to radiation’ under experimental technique, draw the time-temperature plot of the actual observations, the cooling curve and the time-temperature plot of the observations corrected for radiation losses. Note down the value of Tf from this plot.
  9. Using the above observations in the equation: (mw+meq) (T2-Tf) + msss (Ts-Tf)=0. Determine the specific heat capacity of the experimental metal shot.
  10. At the conclusion of the experiment, put the wet shot in the designated place to be dried. Do not put wet shot back in the original container. Empty the water from the boiler and the calorimeter. Dry and spill on the table and floor.
